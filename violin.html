Violin Plot

Overview

The violin plot compares the distribution of concert ticket spending across different music genres. By showing the spread and density of spending for each genre, this visualization allows us to see if certain genres are associated with higher or lower spending on concerts. It also helps identify any outliers or unique patterns in spending behavior that may be tied to specific music preferences.

Analysis

The violin plot compares the distribution of daily music listening hours across different schools. It highlights not only the average listening behavior within each school, but also the spread and concentration of responses. Some schools show a tighter cluster of listening hours, while others have a wider distribution, suggesting more variation in music engagement. Overall, the visualization helps show how listening behavior differs across academic groups while still revealing that most students fall within a similar general range of daily listening time. The violin plot illustrates the distribution of daily music listening hours across different schools, highlighting both the central tendencies and variability within each group. Overall, most students across all schools tend to cluster around moderate listening levels, typically between 2 to 5 hours per day. However, there is noticeable variation in spread, with some schools such as Khoury College of Computer Sciences and the D’Amore-McKim School of Business showing wider distributions and higher upper-range values, indicating the presence of heavier listeners. In contrast, schools like the College of Social Sciences and Humanities display a more concentrated range, suggesting more consistent listening habits among students. These patterns suggest that while general music engagement is relatively similar across schools, certain groups exhibit greater diversity in listening behavior, reflecting differences in lifestyle or student preferences.
