Project Conclusions

Overall Conclusion

Overall, our analysis suggests that college students’ music behavior is connected to concert engagement, but not in a direct or purely predictable way. While students demonstrate clear preferences in genres and artists, these preferences do not translate linearly into spending behavior. Instead, music consumption appears to influence interest and attendance patterns, while actual spending decisions are shaped by a broader set of factors such as affordability, artist demand, and personal priorities. This highlights an important distinction between passive engagement, like listening, and active consumption, like purchasing concert tickets.

Key Findings

Across the visualizations, we observe strong consistency in overall music preferences. Genres such as Pop, R&B, Hip-Hop, Indie, and Electronic dominate across the student population, and artists like Drake, Daniel Caesar, Don Toliver, SZA, and Olivia Dean appear frequently. The treemap and word clouds show that while there are slight variations by school, these differences are relatively small, suggesting that music taste is more universally shared than segmented by academic background. This indicates that student listening behavior is driven more by broader cultural trends than by institutional differences.

At the same time, the Sankey diagram reveals that students often attend concerts aligned with their preferred genres, but not exclusively. There is meaningful cross-genre movement, especially toward widely popular genres like Pop and EDM. This suggests that students are flexible in their concert choices and are willing to explore beyond their stated preferences, likely influenced by social factors or the appeal of live experiences. In contrast, the regression analysis shows only a weak relationship between listening time and concert spending. Even among students with similar listening habits, spending varies widely, reinforcing the idea that engagement alone does not determine willingness to pay.

Why This Matters

These findings have clear implications for the music and entertainment industry. First, they suggest that targeting broad, high-demand genres and artists is an effective way to reach student audiences, since preferences are relatively consistent across groups. However, the presence of cross-genre behavior also indicates that there is value in creating diverse or hybrid concert experiences that appeal to multiple audiences. Additionally, because spending is not strongly tied to listening intensity, pricing strategies should account for factors beyond engagement, such as perceived value, artist popularity, and the overall concert experience.

Final Takeaway

Ultimately, this project demonstrates that while music preferences are useful for understanding student behavior, they are only part of the story. Genre and artist preferences are strong indicators of interest and attendance, but they are not sufficient to explain spending decisions. This suggests that music consumption data is most valuable when combined with additional behavioral and economic factors, rather than being used as a standalone predictor of consumer behavior.

Limitations and Future Work

This analysis is based on a relatively small and localized sample of 101 Northeastern students, which limits the generalizability of the findings. The data was collected through convenience sampling, and some schools may be underrepresented. Future research could improve reliability by expanding the sample size and including students from different universities or geographic regions. Additionally, incorporating variables such as student income, ticket type, concert location, and social influences would provide a more complete understanding of what drives concert attendance and spending behavior.
