Project Conclusions

Overall Conclusion

Overall, our project suggests that college students’ music behavior does relate to concert interest, but not on a one-to-one relationship way. Across the visualizations, students showed clear favorite genres and artists, and those preferences often connected to the kinds of concerts they attended. At the same time, the regression results showed that listening more does not automatically mean someone will spend much more on concert tickets. This suggests that music taste helps explain concert behavior, but spending is also shaped by other factors like budget, artist popularity, and personal priorities.

Key Findings

The word clouds show that Pop, R&B, Hip-Hop, Indie, and Electronic are some of the most popular genres among students, with artists like Drake, Daniel Caesar, Don Toliver, SZA, and Olivia Dean appearing frequently. While there are small differences across colleges, the treemap shows that many students share similar music tastes, with only slight variation by academic group. Overall, students tend to prefer modern and popular genres, suggesting consistent trends across the university.

The Sankey diagram shows that students often attend concerts that match their favorite genres, especially for Pop, EDM, and Hip-Hop, but there is still some movement across different genres. The regression shows a slight positive relationship between listening time and concert spending, but the relationship is weak, meaning listening more does not always lead to spending more. Together, these results suggest that genre and artist preferences are better indicators of concert behavior than listening time alone.

Why This Matters

These findings are useful for the music and entertainment industry because they show that student audiences are shaped by both broad trends and smaller audience differences. For marketing, this means organizers can focus on highly popular genres and artists to reach the widest audience, while also adjusting promotions based on school-specific patterns. For concert managers, the results suggest that audience demand may be stronger when events align with genres students already prefer, but there is still room to attract students through crossover appeal and live-event experiences.

Final Takeaway

In the end, our project does support the idea that college students’ music preferences are fairly connected to the concerts they attend and, to a lesser extent, how much they spend. Genre and artist preferences seem to be stronger indicators of concert behavior than listening time. This means music consumption data can be useful for understanding student audiences, but it works best when combined with other factors rather than treating it as a single indicator of spending.

Limitations and Future

This project's data was collected by convenience and was only completed by 101 Northeastern students on the Boston campus, so the results do not fully represent each college fairly. A larger sample or a broader range of colleges could make the findings stronger. In the future, it would also be helpful to include more variables, such as student budget, ticket type, concert location, and whether or not they are international students. Adding those factors could give a better picture of what actually drives concert spending.
